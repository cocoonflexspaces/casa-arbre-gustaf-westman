The building's story

Once a firehouse. Then an ice cream parlor. Then a furniture store.

For seven days, we'd like to make it a furniture store again.

147 Metropolitan has been many things since the early 20th century, and the building remembers each chapter. Concrete bones, restored. Generous natural light. Industrial proportions softened by warm, tactile finishes — the kind of restoration that doesn't try to hide the layers underneath.

It's a building designed for taking up residence in, not staging in. Which is exactly what a Gustaf Westman pop-up should feel like.

01 The Café · Commercial showcase

A working coffee shop. Your work in everyday commercial life.

The front of Casa Arbre runs as Reforesters Laboratory — an experimental adaptogen café that's already a daily ritual for Williamsburg locals. During the pop-up, the café keeps operating (Option A & B): visitors come in for coffee, encounter your work in commercial use, and leave knowing your name. That's the most authentic discovery moment a designer can engineer.

Your work in actual daily use — on the counter, on the tables, in the booth. The café becomes a Gustaf Westman dispatch: accidental, disarming, exactly the way the people who buy your work first encountered it.

02 The Listening Room · Experiential showcase

The most distinctive room in the building. A 26-channel sound clinic with an indoor garden.

Behind the café, through a passage, is what Reforesters calls the Studio: a soft, low-lit room that runs sound meditations and spatial-audio sessions all week. Layered rugs underfoot, a green wall behind, and a professional 26-independent-channel spatial audio rig — a piece of broadcast-grade infrastructure that's rare in NYC event venues.

Visitors take their shoes off at the door (no-shoe policy), put them in lockers, and step in. It's an inherently slow, considered space — closer to a gallery than a retail floor.

03 The Residence · Lifestyle showcase

Above the café and the listening room, the residential loft.

The top floor is a fully restored apartment with an open layout — kitchen, dining, double-height living — attic-style bedrooms with dormer light, a master bath, and a rear garden you reach through a back door. It's the answer to the question every retail visitor secretly asks: what does this actually look like in someone's home?

For seven days, the answer is: this. A loft you walk through. A dining table set for a small private dinner you might host on day three of the week. A bedroom that looks like someone lives there.

If it were my call

I'd pick Option A — or push hardest for B.

Closing the café (Option C) is the option most clients ask about by default, and the one I'd quietly steer you away from. Here's why: the walk-in coffee customers are your audience. Williamsburg, design-curious, paying $5.50 for a matcha latte at 11am on a Tuesday — that's the exact demographic you'd otherwise pay marketing dollars to find. Closing the café removes the most authentic surface area of the activation.

Option B is the version I'd pitch hardest internally. Gustaf Westman, scored. Furniture and sound, both sourced from craft, programmed together for one week. It's a story Cocoon can tell publicly with you, and the editorial angle that travels furthest beyond Williamsburg.

If your team has a strong reason to want Option C — a closed shoot day, a private dinner you don't want photographed, full control over a particular Tuesday — we can also do a hybrid: A for most of the week, with a single exclusive day inside the booking. Worth a 10-minute call.
